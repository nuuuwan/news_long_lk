The United National Party (UNP) has criticised the government for its move to establish a special court or a Dharmadhikaranaya to resolve issues among the Buddhist clergy, claiming that government cannot establish such a court.

True, but it seems to be a delusion on the part of the UNP if not for an attempt to mislead the country, since President Anura Kumara Dissanayake, during his first announcement on the matter,  said that he accepts the suggestion put forward by the Maha Sangha in respect of a Dharmadhikaranaya to resolve issues being cropped up among monks,  including ones pertaining to discipline.

The suggestion has come from at least a section of the Buddhist monks apparently subsequent to the recent allegations of misbehaviour of certain monks. In fact, the government was also widely accused in social media of dragging its feet in taking action against Atamasthanadhipathi Ven. Pallegama Hemarathana Thera, a leading monk who is out on bail and removed from his high-ranking position after he was accused of sexual abuse.

One solace for the government in this case is that the Opposition, which usually makes a huge fuss at the drop of a hat, has been notably silent on these recent incidents. No party in Opposition has been expressing their views on these incidents or alleged incidents. Nor have they shown any interest in discussing the allegations against the National Child Protection Authority (NCPA) and the police over their lethargic attitude towards these incidents,  which are a main issue debated in the social media. This indifference or  fear at the political level indicates the level of sensitivity the incidents in question carry.

Some sections of  society, express in social media  their reservations to the idea to establish a separate judicial mechanism for the monks, on the grounds that it runs counter to the notion of “One country, one law” which became very popular following the debate over the Muslim Personal law, in the aftermath of the Easter Sunday terrorist attacks of 2019. In fact. President Gotabaya Rajapaksa in October 2021 appointed a contentious Presidential Task Force to study and draft legislation on the “One Country One Law” concept. However, his successor President Ranil Wickremesinghe did not take forward the matter, as the proposal faced pushback from minority communities, legal bodies like the Bar Association of Sri Lanka, and civil rights organisations, over fears of ethnic marginalisation and disregard for democratic processes.

The relationship between the state and  religion has been contentious for centuries in many countries, despite the notion of “Separation of religion from the state”, realisation of it on the ground has always been a question. In some cases,  states have been built with religion as their foundation in countries such as Afghanistan, Iran, Vatican and Israel.

In fact, religion maintains a profound influence on state affairs globally. Countries like United Kingdom, Denmark, Saudi Arabia and Sri Lanka officially endorse a specific religion, granting it privileges, funding, or a primary role in constitutional matters. Although nations such as United States, Turkey and India maintain a formal separation between religion and state, culture and politics still heavily intersect in those countries. In India, Hindutva has almost taken over the government. The 2019 Supreme Court judgment on the Babri Masjid case is a perfectexample of Hindutva influence on the judiciary.

Although Sri Lanka also had a secular Constitution when it was granted Independence in 1948, the first Republican Constitution which was drafted by Dr. Colvin R, de Silva, who was a Marxist,  officially endorsed Buddhism as the primary religion of the country. Now, politicians abuse religion for political gains, particularly during elections, especially under the PR system introduced by the second Republican Constitution.

The Janatha Vimukthi Peramuna (JVP), the core constituent party of the current ruling National People’s Power (NPP) in its first policy declaration published in 1977 strongly promoted the idea of separation of religion from the state. However, NPP has excelled the other main parties now in promoting Buddhist culture.

The government has promised to introduce a new Constitution in three years after its assumption of power. Against this backdrop, it is important for the country’s intellectuals to take over the debate on the relationship between state and religion from social media, where the discussion has been pushed to a vulgar and dangerous level.
