This year, China’s global popularity has risen while that of the US has stumbled, says the latest Pew Research Centre survey. The survey was based on a population of 42,151 adults in 36 countries across the globe. It was conducted between February 8 and May 13 this year.

US President Donald Trump’s off-the-cuff threats, the weaponisation of tariffs and the indecisive Iran campaign have together dented his image and that of the US, vis-à-vis President Xi Jinping and China.

Interestingly, the US is viewed more positively than China in just six of the 36 countries -- India, Japan, the Philippines and South Korea.

In 2025, global views of the United States had worsened as President Donald Trump’s second term began, though most people still had a more positive opinion of the US than of China. But this year, that is no longer the case. For the first time in Pew surveys, China is ahead of the US. Views of China have turned more positive – and views of the US more negative – across 20 of the 36 countries.

Trump’s Troubling Second Term

During the first two years of President Trump’s second term, his ratings worsened significantly. While many people still lack confidence in Xi, positive views of the Chinese President have become more widespread.  More now say that they have confidence in Xi than in Trump.

US Scores on Personal Freedoms

One area where the US still gets higher ratings than China is “personal freedoms.” More say that the US government respects the personal freedoms of its people. Still, the gap has been narrowing – driven by “large drops” in the shares saying the US government respects personal freedoms.

In Sweden, for example, the percentage saying the US respects personal freedoms has declined from 61% in 2021 to 27% today. There were similar drops of 25 percentage points or more in Canada, France, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, South Korea and Spain.

Views Split in Latin America

As China and the US compete for influence around the world, Pew also examined how people in six Latin American countries see their foreign policy roles. People in these countries previously tended to see the US as a more reliable partner than China, but are now split.

The Americans’ nearest neighbours – Canadians and Mexicans – also view China more positively than the US. A majority of Canadians (57%) had a positive view of the US in 2023, while 14% viewed China positively. In 2025, Canadians were equally favourable towards the US and China. But now, more Canadians have a favourable view of China (44%) than of the US (33%). The same pattern is seen in a number of other countries.

Be that as it may, confidence in both Trump and Xi is generally low, though people in many of the 36 countries surveyed view Xi more favourably. In European countries, for example, neither leader gets a majority-positive rating, but views of Xi tend to be more favourable than the views of Trump.

In Germany, Greece, Italy, the Netherlands, Spain, Sweden and the United Kingdom, Xi leads Trump by double digits, though his highest favourability rating is just 37% (in the UK).

In Mexico around a third (35%) say that the Chinese government respects its people’s personal freedoms, while 20% say the same about the US government.

80% of Israelis say the US respects the freedoms of its people, while just 15% say the same of China. Japan shows another sizable difference (61% vs. 6%).  In Bangladesh, Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand, and the West Bank and East Jerusalem, more say China respects personal freedoms than the US.

US Interference in Internal Affairs

Both Chinese and US foreign policies have focused significantly on middle-income countries in recent years. China, for example, has close economic and security ties with Brazil and Peru, among other countries.

People in 17 middle-income countries raise more concerns about the United States’ foreign policy than China’s. A median of 75% say that the US interferes in the affairs of other countries a great deal or a fair amount, while 45% say the same of China. In nearly every country surveyed, more people see the US as an interferer than China.

People in many of these middle-income countries see China as a more reliable partner than the US,  and are more likely to say China contributes to peace and stability around the world, the survey finds.  In South Africa, for example, 72% say China is a reliable partner to their country, while 46% say the same of the US. South Africans are much more likely to say that China (64%) accounts for the interests of countries like their own in making international policy decisions than the US (42%).

In several Latin American countries – Argentina, Brazil, Colombia and Peru – similar shares describe the US and China as reliable partners.

In Pakistan, people are much more likely to say China is a reliable partner than the US (84% vs. 36%). But in the Philippines, many more see the US as a reliable partner than China (81% vs. 42%).

How Newsweek and The Guardian Saw It

Commenting on the survey, “Newsweek” said that China is no less intent on pursuing its security and economic interests, but it couches its policies as highlighting, what it calls, principles of “mutual respect, peaceful coexistence and mutually beneficial cooperation.”

This is the first time in the roughly 20 years Pew has been tracking global opinions that China is viewed more positively than the US. “Views of Beijing and Washington have been very similar at some points in the past but have not been significantly more favourable for China until now,” Laura Silver, associate director of Pew’s Global Attitudes Research told “The Guardian”.

“The US is just not contributing to peace and stability and that is why people have less confidence in Donald Trump,” Silver added. Trump’s demands to control Greenland, the American military raid that captured Venezuela’s then-leader Nicolás Maduro, the barrage of tariffs fired on Canadian goods last year, Trump’s claim that Canada could be “the 51st state of the US”  and the US handling of the Israeli-Hamas war in Gaza have led to low approval in many countries,” Silver explained.

“By comparison, we know that China is seen to be a more reliable partner in many places. It’s more likely to be seen to contribute to global peace and stability,” she added.

People in the US, where about 6 in 10 held positive views of the US in 2023, now view China and the US similarly. Three years ago, the spread was 32 percentage points in Washington’s favour. But the saving grace is that the US is still ahead of China when it comes to government’s respect for personal freedoms, though the gap is shrinking.

The Chinese embassy in Washington told “The Guardian” that the latest poll “demonstrates that China’s governance achievements and development progress are widely recognised.”

The White House is silent on the survey.
