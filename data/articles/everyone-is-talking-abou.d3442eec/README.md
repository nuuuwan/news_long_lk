# Everyone Is Talking About Context for AI. Here’s What Most Companies Still Miss.

[https://hbr.org/sponsored/2026/07/everyone-is-talking-about-context-for-ai-heres-what-most-companies-still-miss](https://hbr.org/sponsored/2026/07/everyone-is-talking-about-context-for-ai-heres-what-most-companies-still-miss)

*01:52 PM, Tuesday, July 21, 2026*

It wasn’t long ago that everyone was speculating about what artificial intelligence (AI) could do. AI would become a personal tutor and a therapist. It would enable fully automated customer service. It would vibecode, write expert copy, and provide deep insights to make everything work better. Utopia was in sight.

More recently, the business conversation has shifted to what AI cannot do. Somewhere in all the excitement about AI pilots and “tokenmaxxing,” reality hit back and got everyone asking: Where’s the return on AI (RoAI)?

Turns out, enterprise leaders might have gotten ahead of themselves. And that has left a large (but solvable) problem to be addressed before AI can fulfill its potential.

Eliminating Operational Blind Spots

Whether you heard it from a coworker or read it in the headlines, you’d think AI agents are making everyone 10 times more productive. But the fact that every employee has access to a large language model does not mean AI will inevitably scale and deliver. Scaling agentic operations on an enterprise level—and getting the massive associated value—is proving to be difficult.

With nearly nine in 10 businesses saying they’re using AI, just 10% say they’re scaling agents successfully, according to McKinsey. Interestingly, leaders paint a far rosier picture of AI returns than those often closer to the work of integration. Metrics of usage may look strong from the top, but the story on the ground is much more complicated. Personal productivity gains are a far cry from an industrialized enterprise solution.

The problem is most AI models are great generalists. Ask AI about outstanding invoices and it will likely give you a perfectly worded description of them, sourced from language patterns it picked up across the internet. But ask it about outstanding invoices in your enterprise from your distributors and you’ll get…

A similarly generic answer, unfortunately.

That’s because data from specific invoices is proprietary, private, and fragmented across internal systems, applications, and devices. Without the ground truth of an enterprise’s operational reality, no AI agent can be trusted to make reliable real-time decisions and take effective actions that drive business outcomes.

That’s why your business needs AI to be a specialist. It needs to know what’s happened, what’s happening now, why it’s happening, and what should happen next—the same things that go through your brain when you’re making a judgment call. And that ability comes from a combination of process data, business knowledge, and decision intelligence.

By eliminating blind spots, this operational context provides AI with total understanding of your business, ensuring AI is deployed strategically and works in harmony with the rest of your people and tools.

Why Operational Context Matters

Let’s revisit the generalist versus specialist framing. Imagine you need to make an important supply chain decision, so you ask your out-of-the-box AI model: “Hey, AI. We’re seeing a spike in demand. Should we reorder?”

Without operational context, it might say something like: “That’s a great question and one I can definitely help with. A spike in demand can certainly result in stockouts. You may want to reorder.” Definitely not the kind of answer you’d use to inform a business-critical decision.

But with your business’s unique operational context, the AI model can be efficient, trustworthy, and effective. It will offer reliable answers, grounded in the reality of your enterprise’s real-time processes and operations. Now ask the same question and you might get a more specific answer, like: “A demand spike in the Northwest region is driving a 75% stockout risk for SKU #103. Based on your existing stock levels, you should reorder. The enterprise resource planning lead time is 10 days, but historical trends show the supplier delivers in 20 days 90% of the time when delivering by rail. I recommend authorizing air freight to reduce delivery volatility. Would you like to complete that authorization now?”

When AI has the operational context that matters, you can trust your agents to take the right actions. You can responsibly scale AI across the enterprise. And you can finally answer the question we started with: Where’s the RoAI?

Enterprise AI and Operational Clarity

AI with context is already achieving impressive results at some industry-leading enterprises.

Fujitsu, one of the world’s oldest technology companies, uses operational context to gain full transparency into inventory levels and AI-enhanced recommendations about when to stock up, reallocate, and refrain from buying materials. The result has been a 20% reduction in excess inventory and multimillion-dollar savings across 10 processes.

Pharmaceutical giant Novo Nordisk uses its operational context to create a roadmap for speeding up clinical development, starting with clinical operations. Today it has more than 100 AI agents speeding up clinical development and expects to reduce its time to market by 12 months—a life-changing timeline for those who rely on its drugs.

“If you don’t have a digital twin of your operation, complete with business context, it’s almost impossible to understand where to deploy AI and automation workflows,” says Stephanie Bova, R&D Digital Transformation Officer at Novo Nordisk.

Everyone is talking about context for AI, but what most companies still miss is that general context isn’t enough—it must be the right operational context. Without a dynamic real-time understanding of how your unique business functions, the massive returns leaders are searching for will remain out of reach.

For relevancy, reliability, and ROAI at scale, AI has to know how your business flows. The context era is here and enterprise leaders can’t afford to miss out on operationalizing it.
