# The Four Business Outcomes That Break When Enterprises Stop Validating Their Defenses

[https://hbr.org/sponsored/2026/06/the-four-business-outcomes-that-break-when-enterprises-stop-validating-their-defenses](https://hbr.org/sponsored/2026/06/the-four-business-outcomes-that-break-when-enterprises-stop-validating-their-defenses)

*07:11 PM, Tuesday, June 30, 2026*

Most large enterprises have built extensive, layered defenses—and most security teams are stretched thin running them. Given those conditions, under a realistic attack, in the environment as it exists today, would those defenses hold together?

This is the gap that compounds: the distance between the defenses an organization has deployed and the resilience it can demonstrate. Some analysts call the accumulated difference “threat debt”: the unresolved exposure that builds quietly until an external shock turns it into a visible liability.

Threat debt matters because the outcomes at risk are not primarily technical. A cyber incident rarely stays contained as a technical event; it becomes an operational disruption, a regulatory exposure, a customer-trust problem, or a material financial loss.

As enterprises digitize more of their operations, cybersecurity increasingly functions as a horizontal layer beneath the business. Operational continuity, customer experience, supply chain resilience, and financial performance all depend on the integrity and availability of digital systems and security teams’ awareness of business context.

Yet most governance models still evaluate cyber risk separately from broader business risk. And while detection and monitoring increasingly run continuously, the work that proves the whole posture holds together—penetration tests, red-team exercises, resilience assessments—still tends to happen on a periodic schedule inherited from an era when environments changed quarterly rather than in real time and regulatory bodies were more accommodating. The defenses are continuous; the assurance that they work often is not.

As that gap widens, four business outcomes begin to deteriorate well before any breach becomes public.

1. Operational Predictability

When one of the largest U.S. health care tech companies suffered a ransomware attack in early 2024, its parent company paid roughly $22 million to the attackers—and still did not recover its data. The ransom turned out to be the smallest part of the cost. The parent company later reported related losses of more than $872 million, with total impact expected to exceed $1 billion.

The cost of recovering from an attack, including downtime, restoration, forensics, and legal expenses, typically rivals or exceeds the ransom itself, according to a Sophos ransomware research report.

The deeper problem is that most organizations lack a reliable way to validate whether their defenses would hold under such attacks before they are tested for real. Digital interdependence creates systemic risk, according to the World Economic Forum’s Global Cybersecurity Outlook: Disruption in one part of an ecosystem can cascade rapidly across suppliers, partners, platforms, and customers.

Modern enterprises operate across interconnected cloud platforms, software-as-a-service (SaaS) applications, application programming interfaces (APIs), third-party identities, and increasingly AI-enabled workflows. Their defensive tooling may run continuously, but too frequently, the assurance that proves it works end to end does not. By the time a periodic assessment concludes, the environment it measured has changed.

And the consequences rarely surface as a security problem first. They show up as the business quietly absorbs unpriced risk: operations that become harder to predict, financial exposure that is harder to quantify, supplier and partner commitments that are harder to stand behind, and plans built on assumptions no one has tested.

Because the exposure sits underneath all these functions at once, leadership tends to experience it not as cyber issues but as a steady erosion of how confidently and quickly the business can move.

2. Decision-Making Quality

False confidence is expensive because it distorts decisions precisely when clarity matters most.

Part of the problem is structural. Executives evaluate resilience through operational continuity, financial exposure, and strategic risk. Security teams often report in findings, vulnerability counts, severity ratings, and patch rates. The two groups are describing the same underlying exposure through different frameworks, and the translation gap leaves leadership answering the wrong question.

Security dashboards are good at reporting how much activity is underway, but not at providing the answer leadership needs: Can we withstand a realistic attack today? Activity metrics can create the appearance of control without validating whether defenses would hold under real conditions, leading organizations to make strategic decisions based on assumed resilience rather than measured resilience.

Customers rarely evaluate security architecture directly, and when they do, they often quantify risk against continually changing targets, primarily evaluating reliability.

Buyers, insurers, investors, and regulators conduct detailed resilience reviews during procurement, renewals, and diligence. A single visible failure like ransomware, an outage, or a vendor compromise can become a data point in how those parties assess operational resilience.

Organizations that cannot demonstrate continuous validation of their defenses face a compounding problem: Each incident that signals fragility erodes trust incrementally, and the downstream effects—customer churn, higher insurance premiums, tighter access to capital—reinforce one another.

4. Strategic Execution

Nearly every executive initiative now carries hidden cyber dependencies. Digital transformation depends on identity resilience. AI adoption depends on identity access and data governance. Mergers and acquisitions (M&A) activity depends on infrastructure integrity. Expansion depends on operational continuity.

As threat debt accumulates, leadership becomes increasingly constrained. Integration timelines slip. Approvals slow. Oversight costs rise. Strategic hesitation grows. What executives experience is unresolved exposure acting as a drag coefficient on growth that surfaces as delayed initiatives, defensive decision making, and reduced competitive agility.

AI Changes Execution, Not Accountability

AI is reshaping both sides of this equation. On one side, AI-assisted development is accelerating the pace of change itself: 65% of developers say at least a quarter of every commit is now generated or shaped by AI, according to Qodo’s 2025 State of AI Code Quality report, and many report far higher proportions. On the other side, attackers are using automation and AI to compress the intervals between reconnaissance, vulnerability discovery, and exploitation across large environments.

AI will also be central to closing the assurance gap, but as a force multiplier for human judgment, not a replacement for it. Its most immediate value is operational scale: accelerating repetitive analysis, monitoring infrastructure changes, mapping potential attack paths, and compressing investigation timelines that once required substantial manual effort. That helps organizations validate exposure at a pace closer to how they operate.

Governance, though, remains fundamentally human. Business leaders still set acceptable risk thresholds. Security teams still weigh trade-offs, prioritize remediation based on control validation, and maintain oversight of how to conduct further validation.

In regulated and high-trust environments, organizations also need auditability, transparency, and confidence in the evidence behind every finding. The organizations seeing the most success are not removing people from the process. They are letting security expertise operate at the speed of modern business.

What Continuous Assurance Looks Like

A growing number of enterprises are already treating resilience validation not as a periodic compliance exercise but as a continuous operational discipline tied directly to business continuity and strategic execution. Rather than determining whether their defenses passed an assessment last quarter, they can answer a sharper question: Do those defenses hold today against the environment as it looks right now?

When assurance becomes continuous, the horizontal exposure and operational tail that once dragged on every part of the business begins to work the other way. Leaders gain a current evidence-based view of where real risk sits and where it does not, so they can prioritize remediation and investment in what genuinely matters. Decisions move faster because they rest on measured resilience rather than assumed resilience. Confidence becomes something an organization can demonstrate on demand to customers, partners, insurers, and regulators. And the initiatives that define growth—transformation, AI adoption, M&A, expansion—can move with less hesitation, because continuous assurance validates the cyber foundation beneath these initiatives as fast as the foundation changes.

Cybersecurity is no longer a vertical discipline managed at the edge of the enterprise or at the end of a project lifecycle. It is becoming a continuous layer of business assurance, embedded across operations, aligned to business risk, and capable of moving at the speed of modern change.

The organizations that implement continuous assurance first will not simply be more secure—they will be more confident, more credible, and more agile because they can validate their defenses’ hold in the environment in real time. That is the primary opportunity for leadership: to turn resilience from an assumption into a capability.
